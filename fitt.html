Fitt 2.0

Where we started.

In 2018, Fitt was a read-only media site built on Wordpress. The site’s main draw was list-style articles that covered the best local restaurants, gyms, outdoor activities, and fitness events. The content spanned over 25 cities and had consistent traffic by way of Google Search.

The founders, Joe and Anthony, secured funding to go from media outlet to full digital product. The challenge was to transform Fitt into an all-in-one platform for living a healthy lifestyle. I joined the company to lead design along with my friend Nick who would lead engineering.

Product Strategy

From what I’ve seen, digital product companies tend to start with a niche utility and at some point decide to create content around said utility (articles, videos, Instagrams, etc.). In our case, we were trying to solve the reverse problem. We had popular content, but no utilities that warranted having actual user accounts. You could already read everything on the site—so what was the point of signing up?

The assumption was that we could find a way to use the content to drive purchases. For example, you could read about the hot new fitness studio and book a class without leaving Fitt. The idea meant convenience for the user, new sales for the businesses, and positioning Fitt as a consumer-friendly marketplace.

My inspiration for how to structure the user experience came from Apple. Around that time, Apple had redesigned their App Store with a content-first approach—articles had become the primary entry point for discovering and downloading apps. If using editorial to drive transactions was working for Apple, it could work for us.

Multi-platform Approach

Early on, we decided that Fitt would be accessible on every platform. For investors, it was important that we started the product overhaul with Fitt’s first iOS app, but we knew we’d eventually have to translate the full experience back to the web where our users already were. At that point, you might as well throw in Android too, right?

Content

Today, the in-house Editorial Team works with writers across 31 cities to document the best places to eat healthy, work out, run, and hike. The most popular content format is Guides: always up-to-date place collections centered around a theme. They also create one-off articles that cover everything from new business openings to interviews with fitness influencers.

Retired Formats

Stories

Yes, we went there. While the Instagram style photo and video slideshows were fashionable, they proved difficult to create on a regular basis and didn’t align with existing user behavior at the time.

News

News was bite-sized editorial snippets inspired by our weekly email newsletter. I still think the format is interesting, but it never really clicked in the product context.

Layer 2

Listings

Places

Places are restaurants, gyms, trails, and run routes extracted from our content. There are over 13k places in our directory that manifest throughout the site as cards, stand-alone pages, and interactive maps.

Emoji-first feedback

We added low-effort user reviews to collect feedback on places. Each review starts with an emoji reaction and ends with an optional “add details” flow that lets us collect information about a place—like if a restaurant offers vegan options or if a gym has an intense atmosphere.

Layer 3

Transactions

With MINDBODY as an investor, it made sense to tackle fitness class booking as the first transactional layer on Fitt. We’re currently working on integrating class schedules via the MINDBODY Affiliate API.

Profiles & Lists

In looking for additional ways to incentivize creating a user profile, we developed Lists—a unique way for Fitt users to create collections of their favorite places.

This ended up being one of our more complicated features as we had to account for:

  • Multiple entry points for adding a place to a List
  • Adding items to an existing List vs. creating a new List mid-flow
  • List annotation and organization
  • Private vs. Public Lists

Signed In vs. Signed Out

As we continued adding more signed-in features, it was important to remember what things looked like for users that had yet to create an account. I introduced inline sign up “blocks” and reactive modals that prompted users to create a profile if they tried bookmarking content or leaving a place review. This approach allows for users to discover product features on their own and avoids promoting sign ups with uninvited popups.

Persistent Elements

City Switcher

While navigating Fitt, users have the ability to switch city context at anytime. This is especially useful for traveling somewhere new.

Cross-content Search

Health and wellness topics can get pretty specific, so it was also important to keep search readily available. Search results are grouped by type—mirroring the top level navigation.

Design System

Our design system continues to mature as the product needs unfold. To maintain consistency and speed, I organize my most-used design elements and styles by category:

  • Assets (Logos)
  • Cards
  • Colors
  • Icons
  • Interface (Buttons, Navigation, Inputs, Avatars)
  • Shadows & Borders
  • Typography

The visual language of Fitt balances friendliness with reliability. I worked with my friend Colin to redraw the original Fitt logo to better encapsulate the overall product aesthetic and brand voice.

Behind the scenes

Custom CMS

We manage all of Fitt with a custom-built CMS. The web app holds information for thousands of articles, places, media assets, and partner ads. Over time, the design has evolved to support new product features and content creation needs.

RIP
